1. Introduction

1.1. Origin of the project

The Holo3-CEMOSIS project was initiated in the frame of the developpement of deflectometers by Holo3. Holo3 was founded in 1986 by a researcher from the ISL ( military research institute located in St-Louis) in the aim to transfer the holography technologies from the defence labs into the industry. As time goes by Holo3 has developped a strong experience in the developpement of techniques for optical metrology industry and a great part of its expertise and buisiness is concentrated in the application of structured light for metrology.

Among the structured light techniques, it can be founded the techniques called deflectometry. These techniques, base on the specular properties of materials, are applied for shiny objects measurement (glasses or waffers measurement for ) .

Figure 1. Deflectometry measurement installation

1.2. Principle of deflectometry for optical measurement

The deflectometry consists in the application of the Snell-Descartes law for reflection in the aim of acquiring geometrical properties of the surface.

The Snell Descartes Law is the following:

Let’s call \overrightarrow{V_i} the incident vector, \overrightarrow{V_r} the reflected vector and \overrightarrow{n} the normal vector, for a given point of the surface. Then the law can be expressed as follows:

  • the three vectors \overrightarrow{V_i}, \overrightarrow{V_r} and \overrightarrow{n} are in the same plane,

  • \overrightarrow{V_r} = 2(\overrightarrow{V_i}\cdot\overrightarrow{n})\overrightarrow{n} - \overrightarrow{V_i}

The Figure 2 represents that law.

Figure 2. Snell Descartes’s law for reflection

It is therefore obvious that from the knowledge of the optical rays (both incident and reflected by the surface) some geometrical characteristics of the shape can be obtained. This is what is done by this techniques used by Holo3.

The principle is to use a digital camera (combination of a sensor and an imaging lens) for which, for every pixel of the sensor the reflected ray is known in a given world referential.

The reflected rays are oriented toward the shiny object to be inspected.

In another part an extended planar pattern is deployed over the object to be inspected. The purpose of this pattern is:

  • to be seen by reflection on the shiny object by the camera,

  • to allow the identification of each point of itself by an appropriate pattern.

For instance, at Holo3, two types of patterns are used:

  • an assembly of white panels with black rectangles and reference LEDs, so that each rectangle can be located into the image,

  • a screen where a structured pattern is displayed.

On the screen the light is modulated spatially, typically by a sinusoidal fringe pattern that is shifted sequentially in the two directions of the screen. Using a well-knowed techniques called phase shifting, a phase can be calculated and associated to a unique pixel (indeed a sub-pixel) of the screen.

1.3. Usage of deflectometry on Polymap

The principle of Polymap is introduced on the Figure 3.

Figure 3. Polymap’s principle

From a calibration of the system, in a referential located in the world, we are able to know for a given pixel I on the image plane the coordinates of the straight line (D). This line (D) is only dependent on the camera and the lens. Thus these lines coordinates are calculated prior to the measurement of the sample.

In a second hand, the position of the pattern inside the world referential is also evaluated prior to any measure by an appropriate calibration procedure.

The measure consists in positionning the sample inside the camera field and to identify the position M onto the screen saw by the pixel I.

The workflow consists in displaying a sinusoidal modulated intensity on the screen, the sensor can measure theses intensities. Then, from these differents intensities a parameter called phase is compute. The phase in the two direction is associated to a point in a referential linked to the screen.

By using the calibration data, point M coordinates can be computed inside the world referential.

Phase Map

Because the sensor is a matrix of pixels, very often we called phase map the image for which the values at each pixel is associated to a phase. The phase is an information that allows to locate the point of the screen viewed by the camera.

Given the two values M and (D) coordinates, one can associate differents configurations of the Surface (S) that respect the Sneel Descartes Law.

Only some position of the point P for a given \vec{n} are acceptable to ensure the respect of the Snell-Descartes law.

Supposing that a surface (S) is considered, defined by a function. Then P and \vec{n} depends of this function and the Snell-Descartes law can be written as a PDE with this function as unknown.

Because this equation is hard to solve at first, beacause non-linear, an approximation is made for the Polymap calculation. It is based on the assumption that the relief is so small that all points are in a planar P_o.

A referential (O,\vec{i},\vec{j},\vec{k}) is identified as the world referential, such as (O,\vec{i},\vec{j} defines P_o.

By this assumption knowing the plane where points are located and the line (D) we know, for the pixel associated to I, the position of the viewed point P in the world referential. By the knowledge of P, I and M, then the vectors \overrightarrow{V_i}, \overrightarrow{V_r} can be directly calculated and thus the \vec{n}.

The position of P is just an hypothesis to allow the determination of \vec{n} , because the surface inspected is in reality not planar. So as to obtain the relief of the surface we perform an integration (using FEM) of the derivative of the relief (compound of \vec{n} ) to get effective relief.

The hypothesis made there is only adapted for very small relief compared to the object/pattern distance, otherwise the \vec{n} hence calculated would be very different from the real one because P position would be false.

1.4. Iterative method for stronger reflief

For stronger reliefs, such as cars glasses, an iterative method is used and is illustrated on Figure 4. In this method the surface is calculated from neighbor to neighbour. For instance, the exact position of the point P1 is known. By the knowledge of M1 we can calculate normal vector of the surface at P1. On P1 a local tangential planar can be determined. If I2 is the neighbor of I1, the position of P2 is the intersection of the planar and (D2). P2 is then known and the same process can be perform for every point of the sensor.

This method is adapted for strong relief but the risk of error propagation is strong.

1.5. Goals of the project

The HOLO3-Cemosis project has two goals, the first one is to improve the FEM implementation used for the POLYMAP surface reconstruction. The second goals is more ambitious and consists in finding a methode to replace both the two methods described upper so as to eliminate the defaults of the Polymap’s hypothesis and the problem of iterative reconstruction. In this context, the resolution of a non-linear PDE obtained from the Snell-Descartes law, could be the solution but it prior implies a string analysis of the problem.
